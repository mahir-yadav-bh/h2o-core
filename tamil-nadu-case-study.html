New Approach to Water Supply | Chennai Case Study



Project Leads

  • Veena Srinivasan
  • Steven Gorelick
  • Larry Goulder

Research Objectives

The research objectives focus on evaluating three distinct solutions to address Chennai's severe water crisis: desalination, efficiency improvement, and rainwater harvesting. The goal is to determine which solution, or combination of solutions, would be most efficient, equitable, and sustainable in resolving the city's water problems.

The key research questions are:

  1. Current Water Usage: How much water is currently being consumed from various sources, for what purposes, and by whom?
  2. Consumer Well-Being: What is the current state of consumer well-being regarding water access, and how can a baseline forecast be developed?
  3. Future Water Demand: How much water will be needed by 2025, considering population growth, income levels, and infrastructure development?
  4. Policy Impact: How will the proposed solutions impact water consumption, distribution, and sustainability for different consumer groups?

This research seeks to provide a clear framework for evaluating the most effective strategies to address Chennaiâ€™s water challenges.

Research Method

To meet the research objectives, an integrated dynamic simulation model for the Chennai basin was developed. This model links user behaviour, utility operations, and basin-scale water resources. It was run for two periods:

  1. Historical Run (2002-2006): Calibrated the model and provided insights into Chennai's water system.
  2. Forecasting Run (2007-2025): Projected the water supply situation in 2025 and tested policy impacts.

Simulations

Historical Run Overview

The integrated model was applied from January 2002 to January 2006, covering a multi-year drought (2003-2004) and a year of record rainfall (2005). The results were analysed using two climatic extremes as reference points: a dry period (Jan-Apr 2004) and a wet period (Jan-Apr 2006) following the record rains.

Key Findings

The historical run revealed that Chennai's reservoir system was capacity-constrained, leading to highly variable and intermittent utility water supply. During shortages, consumers increasingly relied on private and community wells. As groundwater extraction rose and recharge rates declined, aquifer levels dropped, causing wells to dry up. This forced consumers to purchase expensive tanker water, leading to significant losses in well-being. The model effectively quantified these impacts.

Chennai 2025 Baseline Scenarios

The model was projected to 2025 by accounting for changes in population, income, and land use. A desalination plant with a capacity of 100 million litres per day, assumed to start operating in 2009, was factored into the model. Various rainfall scenarios were simulated using historical data from 2008-2025.

Key Findings

  1. Urbanization Impact: Contrary to expectations, the shift from irrigated agriculture due to increased urbanization did not result in additional water availability for urban uses. Instead, the growing population, along with commercial and industrial expansion, absorbed much of the water that would have otherwise gone to agriculture.
  2. Desalination Plant Impact: The introduction of the desalination plant did not significantly mitigate the effects of a multi-year drought. The utility supply still faced shutdowns, leading to aquifer depletion and a surge in demand for costly tanker water. However, the plant did help the utility keep pace with rising demand from population and income growth, preventing the situation from worsening further.

Chennai 2025 Policy Scenarios

The model projected Chennai's water situation to 2025 by considering changes in population, income, and land use, along with different rainfall scenarios using historical data from 2008-2025. Three policy scenarios were analysed:

  1. Supply Augmentation: This involved increasing reservoir capacity by 50% and adding a second desalination plant with a capacity of 100 million litres per day.
  2. Efficiency Improvement: This scenario focused on raising tariffs for piped water supply and reducing leakage in the pipelines.
  3. Rainwater Harvesting: This policy aimed at improving aquifer recharge through rooftop rainwater harvesting and community-based stormwater harvesting efforts.

These scenarios were compared to assess their effectiveness in addressing Chennai's future water challenges.

Key Insights from the Model:

The model accurately replicated Chennaiâ€™s 2003-2004 drought, showing reservoirs dried up, piped supply shut down, and consumers increasingly relied on private wells, which led to the aquifer drying up. This forced residents to buy costly tanker water, causing significant distress.

The research suggests that Chennai's severe water crisis could recur, even with a new desalination plant, due to the city's limited reservoir capacity and rainfall patterns. While building more desalination plants would increase water availability, it is not cost-effective given current technology and energy prices.

More viable solutions include raising water tariffs, fixing leaks, and implementing rainwater harvesting. These cost-effective measures could significantly alleviate future water crises.

Additionally, the research proposes a "dual-quality" water system, where high-quality, metered water is provided for essential uses, while private wells supply water for less critical purposes. This approach could help balance water availability and costs during shortages.
