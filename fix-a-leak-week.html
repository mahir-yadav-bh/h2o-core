Fix a Leak Week



Fix a Leak Week, organized by the EPA’s WaterSense program, is an annual event dedicated to educating homeowners about the importance of fixing household leaks. The campaign highlights how small, easily fixable leaks can waste significant amounts of water—up to 10,000 gallons annually per household—and result in higher water and energy bills.

Importance of Fixing Leaks

Leaks are a major source of water waste in homes across the U.S., often going unnoticed until they significantly impact utility bills. According to the EPA, household leaks account for nearly 1 trillion gallons of wasted water nationwide each year. Fixing leaks not only conserves water but also reduces the energy required to heat, pump, and treat water, leading to lower utility costs.

Common Household Leaks

1. Dripping Faucets: A dripping faucet, often caused by worn washers or gaskets, can waste thousands of gallons of water annually. Even a slow drip at one drop per second can waste over 3,000 gallons of water a year. Fixing a dripping faucet usually requires replacing the washer or cartridge, which is a simple and inexpensive repair.

2. Running Toilets: Running toilets are another common source of water waste. A faulty flapper valve, which controls the flow of water from the tank to the bowl, is usually the culprit. This can be easily tested by adding a few drops of food coloring to the tank; if color appears in the bowl within 10 minutes without flushing, there is a leak. Replacing the flapper is a straightforward fix that can save significant amounts of water.

3. Leaky Showerheads: A showerhead leaking at 10 drips per minute can waste more than 500 gallons of water annually—enough water to wash 60 loads of dishes in a dishwasher. Tightening connections and using pipe tape can often solve this problem.

4. Outdoor Leaks: Outdoor leaks, such as those from irrigation systems or spigots, can also contribute to water waste. Checking for damp spots on the lawn, especially during dry weather, or inspecting the garden hose for leaks at its connection to the spigot can help identify these issues. Replacing worn hose connections and repairing or replacing damaged irrigation components can prevent significant water loss.

How to Detect Leaks

1. Check Your Water Meter: One of the most effective ways to determine if you have a leak is to check your water meter. Turn off all water sources in your home and check the meter reading. Wait for two hours without using water, then check the meter again. If the reading has changed, you likely have a leak.

2. Inspect Your Bill: A sudden spike in your water bill without a corresponding increase in water usage can indicate a leak. Comparing your current bill with past bills can help identify potential leaks.

3. Use the Toilet Dye Test: As mentioned earlier, placing a few drops of food coloring in your toilet tank and waiting 10 minutes without flushing can help identify leaks. If the color appears in the bowl, the flapper valve needs to be replaced.

4. Listen and Look: Listening for running water when no faucets are on or visually inspecting under sinks and around appliances like washing machines and dishwashers for pooling water can also help detect leaks.

Benefits of Fixing Leaks

Fixing leaks can have a significant impact on water conservation efforts. By addressing leaks, households can save about 10% on their water bills, amounting to an average savings of $10 per month or more, depending on water rates. The collective impact of millions of households fixing leaks can contribute to preserving local water supplies, especially in areas facing drought or water scarcity.

Moreover, fixing leaks reduces the strain on wastewater treatment facilities. Less water wasted means less water needs to be treated, leading to energy savings and reduced greenhouse gas emissions. This contributes to a more sustainable and environmentally friendly community.

Engaging in Fix a Leak Week

Fix a Leak Week is an excellent opportunity for communities, schools, and organizations to engage in water conservation efforts. The EPA encourages participation through local events, workshops, and social media campaigns. Homeowners can take part by inspecting their homes for leaks, conducting simple repairs, and educating others about the importance of water conservation.

Communities can host events such as leak detection workshops, and organize neighborhood water audits. Schools can incorporate lessons on water conservation into their curriculum and encourage students to conduct leak inspections at home.

Long-Term Water Conservation

Fix a Leak Week is just the beginning of a year-round commitment to water conservation. By regularly inspecting your home for leaks, using WaterSense-labeled products, and practicing water-efficient behaviors, you can contribute to the long-term sustainability of our water resources. Simple actions, like turning off the tap while brushing your teeth, using a broom instead of a hose to clean driveways, and watering lawns in the early morning or late evening, can make a big difference.

By making water conservation a habit, you can help ensure that there is enough water to meet the needs of future generations while also saving money and energy today.
