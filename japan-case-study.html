Toilet Flushing & Landscape Irrigation | Japan Case Study



Introduction

Water reuse has become a crucial strategy worldwide for sustainable water management. It helps conserve water resources, mitigate environmental impacts from wastewater discharge, and reduce the costs and energy demands of traditional water management practices. While many regions implement water reuse due to water scarcity, Japan has adopted this approach driven by its unique challenges in water resource management and environmental needs.

Japan's Water Resource Challenges

Japan, with its wet and mild climate, receives an average annual precipitation of 1,718 mm—over twice the global average. However, despite this abundance, the availability of freshwater per capita is significantly lower in Japan than in many other countries. Due to the country's small land area, rapid river flows, and seasonal rainfall patterns, Japan’s freshwater availability is only about 3,300 m³ per person per year, less than half the global average of 7,800 m³. This limited availability has posed significant challenges, particularly in densely populated urban areas.

During the period of rapid economic growth from the late 1950s to the early 1970s, Japan faced severe water stress in its major cities, exacerbated by frequent droughts. These challenges highlighted the need for alternative water sources. In response, Japan began implementing water reclamation and reuse, especially in urban areas. For instance, Fukuoka, after experiencing a severe drought in 1978, adopted reclaimed water for urban uses like toilet flushing. Similarly, Tokyo began using reclaimed water during its urban redevelopment, implementing systems on a block-wide or building scale to provide water for non-potable uses such as landscape irrigation.

Environmental Protection as a Key Driver

Environmental protection has been a significant motivator for water reuse in Japan. The rapid urbanization of the 1980s led to a dramatic decrease in the flow of urban streams, causing environmental degradation. The Tokyo metropolitan government responded by using reclaimed water to replenish these dry streams, restoring stream flow and improving the aquatic environment. The success of this initiative in Tokyo led to the adoption of similar practices across the country, making stream flow augmentation the most common use of reclaimed water in Japan today.

Water Reuse for Disaster Preparedness

Japan’s vulnerability to natural disasters, particularly earthquakes, has also driven the adoption of water reuse as a strategy for disaster preparedness. The 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake severely damaged many water and wastewater treatment plants, leading to water supply restrictions. This event highlighted the importance of alternative water sources during emergencies. Onsite water reuse systems, in particular, have gained recognition for their potential to provide reliable water supplies during such crises.

Government Initiatives to Promote Water Reuse

Recognizing the importance of water reuse, the Japanese government has enacted several key laws to promote its implementation. In 2014, the Basic Act on the Water Cycle became Japan’s first law to emphasize water reuse as a critical component of water resource management. That same year, the government introduced the New Sewage Vision, outlining a plan to double the number of water reclamation facilities in cities with populations over 100,000. In 2015, the Water Resources Policy further strengthened the government’s commitment to promoting water reuse.

Despite these efforts, the adoption of reclaimed water in Japan has been limited by two main challenges: the lack of stringent quality standards and the high energy consumption of water reclamation facilities. Although guidelines were established in 2005 covering parameters such as E. coli, turbidity, and residual chlorine, they do not adequately address chemical and pathogen risks. Establishing comprehensive quality standards is essential to ensuring the safety of reclaimed water and gaining public trust.

Moreover, improving water quality often involves trade-offs with cost and energy consumption. This necessitates the development of efficient water reclamation processes tailored to specific applications.

Technological Advancements in Water Reuse

To address these challenges, Japan has invested in research to develop energy-efficient water reclamation technologies. One notable initiative was the Core Research for Evolutional Science and Technology (CREST) project, conducted from 2010 to 2015. This project focused on advanced membrane technologies and ozonation to improve the performance and economic feasibility of water reclamation. A key outcome was the development of an ultrafiltration plus ultraviolet radiation (UF + UV) process for treating water for agricultural reuse. This process achieved over 5.2-log removal of viruses at a pilot-plant scale, with lower cost and energy consumption compared to traditional methods.

Building on this success, a feasibility study was conducted under the Breakthrough by Dynamic Approach in Sewage High Technology (B-DASH) project from 2015 to 2017. A demonstration plant using the UF + UV process was constructed at the Itoman wastewater treatment plant in Okinawa to evaluate its effectiveness. The reclaimed water was used for agricultural irrigation, and the vegetables grown were sold locally. Surveys highlighted the importance of public education and outreach in promoting water reuse.

Future Perspectives

Japan’s experience with water reuse reflects a broader global trend toward sustainable water management. As Japan continues to refine its technologies and expand their applications, it serves as a model for other nations facing similar challenges. Future efforts are likely to focus on overcoming barriers like enhancing water quality standards and reducing energy consumption. With ongoing research and innovation, Japan is well-positioned to lead in sustainable water management through advanced water reuse systems.

In 1978, Fukuoka City faced a severe drought that led to 287 days of water supply restrictions. This crisis prompted the city to pioneer Japan’s first regional water reuse scheme, aiming to establish a “water conservation-conscious city.” The Fukuoka city government, in collaboration with its citizens, began promoting the installation of water recycling facilities to treat wastewater for non-potable uses like toilet flushing and landscape irrigation.

The first significant development in this initiative was the installation of a water reclamation facility at the Chubu wastewater treatment plant in 1980, near the city center. Initially, the facility had a capacity of 400 cubic meters per day, using sand filtration, ozonation, and chlorination to treat the secondary effluent. The treated water was distributed through a dual pipeline system, with the reclaimed water pipes marked in yellow to distinguish them from the potable water supply. This reclaimed water was first provided to 12 public facilities, including the city hall and the central police station, primarily for toilet flushing.

As demand for reclaimed water increased, Fukuoka expanded the capacity of its water reclamation facility and improved the quality of the treated water. Over time, additional treatment processes, such as coagulation, sand filtration, pre-filtration, ozonation, and chlorination, were incorporated to enhance water quality. By 1989, the reclaimed water network had extended to large buildings in the city center, and by 1995, it reached commercial areas.

The city’s vulnerability to water shortages was highlighted again during another severe drought in 1994. In response, a second water reclamation facility was constructed in 2003 at the Tobu wastewater treatment plant. Additionally, the Fukuoka city government introduced an ordinance requiring building owners to install water reclamation facilities. The city’s water reuse project was funded by a national subsidy and through reclaimed water rates charged to users.

Meanwhile, Tokyo also recognized the importance of water reuse in the 1980s, particularly in the west Shinjuku district, where a large urban redevelopment project was underway. The Tokyo Metropolitan Government encouraged water reuse to reduce the need for extensive water and wastewater infrastructure investments. New buildings were required to install water recycling systems, including dual distribution systems for reclaimed water. The Ochiai wastewater treatment plant supplied reclaimed water for toilet flushing and green belt irrigation in this district. The government’s efforts led to the adoption of water reuse systems in several key areas of Tokyo.

However, the expansion of regional water reuse in Tokyo remained limited to just three areas: Shinjuku, Shinagawa, and Tokyo Bay. Building owners outside these regions faced the financial burden of installing and maintaining individual recycling systems or rainwater harvesting systems, which were costly and less reliable. Consequently, the adoption of water reuse facilities in Tokyo was limited, and many users of rainwater harvesting systems often had to rely on the public water supply during droughts.

Fukuoka City and Tokyo’s early initiatives in water reuse demonstrated the potential for sustainable urban water management in Japan. However, challenges such as limited regional coverage and the financial burden on building owners have slowed the broader adoption of these practices. Despite these challenges, the efforts made by Fukuoka and Tokyo laid important groundwork for future water conservation and reuse initiatives in Japan.
