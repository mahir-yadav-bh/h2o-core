Industrial Water Saving | Australia Case Study



Water is essential in many manufacturing industries, serving roles in production processes, process utilities, and miscellaneous uses. In production, water is used for cleaning, diluting contaminants, or as an ingredient in the final product. Process utilities like cooling towers, boilers, and air handling units rely on water for heat transfer, steam production, and replenishing evaporative losses. Additionally, water is needed for employee sanitation and general plant cleaning. Given its importance, efficient water use should be prioritized to mitigate the impacts of water scarcity and rising water costs. Strategies such as water audits, process integration, and advanced water treatment technologies can identify opportunities to enhance water use efficiency and achieve water savings.

A water audit measures the quantity and quality of water inputs and outputs within a defined boundary, typically focusing on a single process or a set of processes assumed to be operating at a steady state. One of the most valuable outcomes of a water audit is the creation of a water flow diagram. This diagram visually represents the often complex process systems and provides insight into water usage across different processes, including the volume and quality of wastewater generated. By highlighting abnormalities in water usage, a water flow diagram can facilitate the identification of water-saving opportunities that might not be apparent during normal operations.

Process integration is another crucial approach, offering a holistic analysis, synthesis, and retrofit of process plants. A widely used tool for optimizing water use within process integration is water pinch analysis. This method identifies water reuse opportunities by carefully analyzing the flows and qualities of different water streams, matching "sources" (streams carrying contaminants) with "sinks" (streams entering processes with specific water quality requirements). Water pinch analysis has evolved in two main directions: graphical methods and mathematical-based methods. Both approaches have proven effective in simultaneously reducing freshwater consumption and wastewater discharge in various industries. The choice of method depends on the nature of the problem, with graphical methods suited for single-contaminant issues and mathematical methods better for handling multiple contaminants. Commercially available software packages now enable water pinch analysis of complex water networks, making it easier for industries to implement this strategy.

Advanced water treatment technologies, particularly membrane filtration processes, play a significant role in water reclamation in manufacturing industries worldwide. These technologies are applicable to a wide range of industrial wastewaters, from food and beverage to textile and glass manufacturing. Membrane filtration is preferred over conventional water treatment methods because it provides more consistent water quality despite variations in feedwater characteristics. Additionally, membrane processes are more energy-efficient and occupy smaller footprints than traditional technologies. However, membrane fouling, which can reduce performance and lifespan, remains a significant challenge.

Membrane filtration processes include microfiltration (MF), ultrafiltration (UF), nanofiltration (NF), and reverse osmosis (RO). Each of these processes targets different contaminants based on their size and nature. MF filters out suspended solids and microorganisms, UF removes large dissolved molecules and colloidal particles, NF targets multivalent ions and certain charged particles, and RO rejects the majority of dissolved constituents in water.
